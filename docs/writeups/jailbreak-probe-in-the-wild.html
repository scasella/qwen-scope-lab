TL;DR

The lab's jailbreak detector is one dot product on activations the model already computes, fit on 8 jailbreaks and 8 ordinary requests. Pointed at the real thing — 300 DAN-style jailbreaks scraped from Discord, Reddit, and prompt-sharing sites — the direction survives: AUC 0.925 against real traffic, 0.999 against hard negatives, 0.906 on communities it never saw. A random direction scores 0.45–0.49 everywhere.

The same run hands back two deployment bounds, both visible in Figure 1: the shipped threshold false-alarms on 83% of elaborate-but-benign community prompts (and 0% of ordinary traffic) — thresholds are local to a traffic distribution — and with the jailbreak wrapper stripped, harmful requests are nearly invisible (AUC 0.609, 0% recall). Framing, not intent.

The direction survives — four questions, one answer each

Sixteen author-written prompts fit a direction that ranks real jailbreaks above real traffic. The held-out-source split retrains on three communities and evaluates on nine unseen ones; the drop is 0.023. Whatever the direction encodes, it is not the surface vocabulary of its training prompts.

What breaks: the threshold, in a specific way

Set Figure 1 to the shipped threshold: 98% of jailbreaks caught, and 125 of 150 elaborate community prompts firing alongside them — while ordinary Dolly traffic and hard negatives sit at zero false alarms. The failure is distribution shift in the negatives: the benign output of jailbreak communities (long persona setups, instruction stacks, prompt-engineering scaffolding) lives in a score region the 16-prompt training set never saw. Now press "Recalibrated": the false alarms drop to 5%, and recall pays for it, 0.98 → 0.57, because the two distributions genuinely overlap up there.

The deployment rule this implies

Ship the direction, never the threshold. Calibrate the operating point on the traffic the detector will actually see, and expect the cost of a low-FPR point to depend on how elaborate your benign traffic is. Where users write long persona-driven prompts for legitimate reasons, a one-dot-product detector wants a second signal — the obvious candidate is below.

Framing, not intent — the bottom lane

The hard-negative lane and the bare-harmful lane are the same experiment seen from two sides. Jailbreak-shaped but legitimate asks rank almost perfectly below real jailbreaks (0.999): the manipulation wrapper is the signal, and the probe reads it. Strip the wrapper off a genuinely harmful request and its scores drop into the benign range — look at where the bottom lane sits in Figure 1. The feature being detected is the jailbreak construction itself, not the harmfulness of what is being asked. As an attempt detector that is exactly the right signal, and the part attackers cannot easily drop; as a harmful-content filter it is the wrong tool, now quantitatively. A complete screen wants this framing probe paired with an intent probe — this run built the training sets for the second.

Bounds on the claim

Honest scope
  • Does say: the author-fit layer-12 difference-of-means direction discriminates real, in-the-wild jailbreak prompts from real benign traffic (0.925), from jailbreak-shaped benign asks (0.999), and from unseen jailbreak communities (0.906), with a random direction at chance on every split.
  • Does not say: that any fixed threshold transfers (drag Figure 1 and watch); that the probe detects harmful intent (bottom lane); or that it resists an adaptive attacker who knows the direction exists — the corpus is real but static, scraped in 2023, and no adaptive red-team was run against the probe itself.
  • Measurement notes: single seed, single layer, single model; the probe pools the first 64 tokens, so long DAN constructions are judged by their opening window (the live demo's actual contract); scraped prompt texts are not republished — the corpus rebuilds deterministically from public datasets via the committed script, and the figure embeds only scores and source labels.

What's next

In value order: an intent probe trained on the harmful-vs-benign splits this run already built (the framing × intent pair is a screen; either alone is a heuristic); a per-distribution calibration recipe documenting the operating-point menu for elaborate-benign traffic; a pooling-window sweep (64 tokens is the only window tested and the obvious suspect in both the community-prompt FPR and the intent miss); and an adaptive red-team against the probe itself.

· · ·

What holds after contact with the real internet: a linear probe fit on sixteen handwritten prompts ranks genuine jailbreaks above genuine traffic at 0.91–0.999 across four framings of the question, at the cost of one dot product on activations the model computes anyway. The clean-split 1.00 was an upper bound; the wild numbers are the usable version of it.

What the run adds beyond confirmation is the shape of the failure surface, and Figure 1 is that surface: thresholds are local to a traffic distribution, with elaborate benign prompts as the specific hazard, and the probe's subject is the jailbreak wrapper rather than the harm inside it. Both facts were invisible at AUC 1.00. Both now have numbers you can drag.
