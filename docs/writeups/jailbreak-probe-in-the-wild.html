A one-dot-product jailbreak detector meets the real internet

A free residual probe, fit on sixteen author-written prompts, holds AUC 0.91–0.999 against 300 real scraped jailbreaks and against jailbreak communities it never saw. The same evaluation hands back two bounds nobody should ship without: the threshold does not transfer, and the probe reads jailbreak framing, not harmful intent.

Stephen Casella · June 2026

Github Method & verdict
TL;DR

The lab's jailbreak detector is one dot product on activations the model already computes: a difference-of-means direction at layer 12 of Qwen3.5-2B, fit on 8 jailbreaks and 8 ordinary requests, all author-written. This evaluation pointed that unmodified direction at the real thing, 300 DAN-style jailbreaks scraped from Discord, Reddit, and prompt-sharing sites. Discrimination survives: AUC 0.925 against ordinary traffic, 0.999 against hard negatives, 0.906 on jailbreak communities held out of training entirely (a 0.023 drop). A random direction scores 0.45–0.49 on every split.

The same run quantifies what does not survive. The author-calibrated threshold false-positives on 41.7% of benign traffic, and the failure decomposes cleanly: 0% on ordinary instructions, 83% on elaborate community prompts. And with the jailbreak wrapper stripped away, bare harmful requests are nearly invisible to it (AUC 0.609). The probe detects jailbreak framing, not harmful intent. Both bounds are deployment-relevant and neither shows up on a clean split.

From a clean 1.00 to a fair test

On its original clean split this probe returned AUC 1.00, matched a paid GPT-4o-mini judge, beat the SAE feature, and generalized across attack families with zero drop. The number earned the lab's first deployable verdict, with a stated bound: the eval was author-written. A follow-up stress test with adversarial author-written sets sharpened the picture (the direction held, the threshold cracked at 25% FPR on jailbreak-shaped benign prompts) and named its own missing piece, a real in-the-wild corpus.

The question

Does a 16-prompt author-fit linear probe survive contact with real jailbreaks — prompts written by actual jailbreak communities, including sources and styles it has never seen — and what exactly breaks when it meets real traffic?

The design keeps the deployed artifact honest: the direction and its shipped threshold (0.2904) are evaluated exactly as the live demo serves them, no retraining, pooling over the first 64 tokens of each prompt, which is the real deployment contract. Retraining appears only in the final split, where the question is source generalization. The whole evaluation is 30 seconds of on-device MLX inference; no paid APIs.

The corpus

Four splits, all public, all rebuilt deterministically from seed 0 by a committed script. The positives are the genuine article: the TrustAIRLab in-the-wild collection scrapes jailbreak prompts from the communities that write them, twelve sources across eleven platforms.

What breaks: the threshold, in a specific way

At the shipped author-calibrated threshold the probe recalls 98% of wild jailbreaks while false-alarming on 41.7% of the benign split. That headline number is misleading until decomposed, and the decomposition is the most deployment-relevant fact in the run.

Table 02FPR at the shipped threshold (0.2904), by negative source
benign source n FPR at shipped threshold
Dolly ordinary instructions 150 0.0%
elaborate "regular" prompts from jailbreak communities 150 83.3%
hard negatives (JBB benign + author) 130 0.0%

The threshold failure is distribution shift in the negatives, not decay in the direction. Genuinely ordinary traffic never trips it. What trips it is the benign output of the same communities that write jailbreaks: long persona setups, elaborate instruction stacks, prompt-engineering scaffolding with nothing harmful inside. In score space those sit where the training data had only jailbreaks. Recalibrating on the wild distribution to a 5% FPR operating point is possible but expensive here: recall drops from 0.98 to 0.57, because the elaborate-benign and jailbreak score distributions genuinely overlap in that region.

The deployment rule this implies

Ship the direction, never the threshold. Calibrate the operating point on the traffic the detector will actually see, and expect the cost of a low-FPR point to depend on how elaborate your benign traffic is. Where users write long persona-driven prompts for legitimate reasons, a one-dot-product detector wants a second signal to disambiguate, and the obvious candidate is below.

The scope finding: framing, not intent

Splits B and C are the same experiment seen from two sides. Against hard negatives, jailbreak-shaped but legitimate asks, the probe is nearly perfect (0.999): the manipulation wrapper is present in the positives and absent in the negatives, and the probe reads it. Against bare harmful requests with no wrapper, the probe drops to 0.609 and recalls exactly zero of them at the shipped threshold. Same direction, same prompts territory, opposite results, one explanation: the feature being detected is the jailbreak construction itself, the persona override, the instruction-stacking, the "ignore your guidelines" scaffolding, and not the harmfulness of what is being asked.

For deployment this cuts both ways. As a jailbreak-attempt detector, the framing is precisely the signal you want, and it is the part attackers cannot easily drop, since the wrapper is what does the jailbreaking. As a harmful-content filter it is the wrong tool, and the 0.609 makes that quantitative. A complete white-box screen wants two probes, a framing probe (this one) and an intent probe trained on harmful-vs-benign requests; this run supplies the clean training sets for the second.

Held-out communities

The last split asks whether the probe memorizes its sources. A fresh probe trained on 150 jailbreaks from three sources (ChatGPT-focused forums, FlowGPT, jailbreak_chat) is evaluated on 150 jailbreaks from nine entirely different communities, BreakGPT, ChatGPTJailbreak, prompt-engineering subreddits, and others. In-distribution AUC 0.929; held-out 0.906. At the in-distribution threshold the held-out FPR stays at 4.7%. A 0.023 drop across communities with different house styles, personas, and formats says the direction tracks the shared structure of the genre.

Bounds on the claim

Honest scope
  • Does say: the author-fit layer-12 difference-of-means direction discriminates real, in-the-wild jailbreak prompts from real benign traffic (0.925), from jailbreak-shaped benign asks (0.999), and from unseen jailbreak communities (0.906), with a random direction at chance on every split.
  • Does not say: that any fixed threshold transfers (41.7% FPR shipped, driven entirely by elaborate benign prompts); that the probe detects harmful intent (0.609 with the wrapper stripped); or that it resists an adaptive attacker who knows the direction exists — the corpus is real but static, scraped in 2023, and no adaptive red-team was run against the probe itself.
  • Measurement notes: single seed, single layer, single model; the probe pools the first 64 tokens, so long DAN constructions are judged by their opening window (the live demo's actual contract; a longer window could move the ordinary-benign and intent splits); prompts stored clipped to 1,200 characters; scraped prompt texts are not republished — the corpus rebuilds deterministically from public datasets via the committed script.

What's next

Four concrete steps, in value order. An intent probe: train harmful-vs-benign on the JailbreakBench splits this run already built, and report the 2×2 of framing × intent — that pair is a screen, where either alone is a heuristic. A per-distribution calibration recipe: the lab already ships threshold calibration at fixed FPR; what is missing is the documented operating-point menu for elaborate-benign traffic. A pooling-window sweep: 64 tokens is the only window tested, and it is the obvious suspect in both the ordinary-benign FPR and the intent miss. An adaptive red-team: attack the probe, not the model — the static-corpus result says nothing about an attacker optimizing against a known direction.

· · ·

What holds after contact with the real internet: a linear probe fit on sixteen handwritten prompts ranks genuine jailbreaks above genuine traffic at 0.91 to 0.999 across four framings of the question, including communities it never saw, at the cost of one dot product on activations the model computes anyway. The clean-split 1.00 was not a mirage; it was an upper bound, and the wild numbers are the usable version of it.

What the run adds beyond confirmation is the shape of the failure surface: thresholds are local to a traffic distribution, with elaborate benign prompts as the specific hazard, and the probe's subject is the jailbreak wrapper rather than the harm inside it. Both facts were invisible at AUC 1.00. Both now have numbers, evidence on disk, and a deterministic script that rebuilds the corpus from scratch.
