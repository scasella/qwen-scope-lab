TL;DR

Concept-manifold steering makes a path claim: moving along the fitted manifold should pass through a concept's intermediate values, where a straight activation-space line jumps to the endpoint. On the arousal axis of a real Qwen3.5-2B, the manifold walk induces all six emotions in order (agreement 1.00); the linear steer scores 0.33, a random direction at matched norm 0.17 (chance), and a manifold fitted with the emotion order shuffled 0.83.

The win is concept-shaped, and the two failures say why: a valence line collapses where it must cross the negative-to-positive sign flip, and a fear line never moves at all. One seed, one model, three concepts, all on-device via MLX.

The claim, in one picture

Goodfire's manifold-steering work showed that ordinal concepts trace low-dimensional curves in a model's residual stream, and this lab replicated that geometry on Qwen3.5-2B (isometry r = 0.94–1.00 across seven concepts). Emotions are the natural next family, and they make the path claim vivid: Figure 1's curve is the fitted arousal manifold, and the chord is what ordinary linear steering does. Look at where the chord travels: it leaves "numb", crosses empty activation space far from every intermediate centroid, and lands near "frantic". The spline goes the long way round because that is where the emotions actually live.

The question

At waypoint k of a 6-step steer from "numb" to "frantic", is the model's induced top prediction the k-th intermediate emotion? We call the testable version centroid routing. Behavior is read out per waypoint as the model's distribution over the six emotion words under the active intervention.

How a walk works — the method in four moves

01 · capture Six points in activation space For each emotion word, average the layer-12 residual over carrier sentences ("She felt numb", "A numb mood", …). One centroid per emotion.
02 · fit A spline through them PCA, then a cubic spline through the centroids in order — the manifold. The straight source→target segment is the linear chord it competes against.
03 · walk Replace and regenerate At each of six waypoints, overwrite the emotion token's residual with the path point — manifold or chord — and let the model continue from there.
04 · read out Ask it how it feels The induced distribution over the six emotion words. Its argmax is the waypoint's verdict; its distance to the behavior manifold is the energy.

The controls carry the claim

Routing through intermediates could be cheap. If the carrier prompt biases the model toward nearby emotion words, any on-distribution wobble might light them up. Two controls run on the same prompt and norm budget rule that out.

Two failures, two mechanisms — also visible in the geometry

The same protocol on the other two emotion lines does not produce routing wins, and you can see why in their shapes.

Bounds on the claim

Honest scope
  • Does say: on one clean monotone emotion axis, with the ordering hypothesis fixed in advance, steering along the fitted manifold reproduces the full ordered sequence of intermediate states where the straight chord, matched-norm noise, and a shuffled ordering do not. The geometry gate (isometry r ≥ 0.9) passes on all three emotion concepts.
  • Does not say: that manifold steering beats linear steering on emotions generally (1 of 3 concepts), that the routing survives more seeds or prompt variation (n = 1 walk per arm, single seed, single model), or anything about downstream behavior such as compliance. The lab's earlier work refuted the affection-induces-compliance coupling on this model under two judges; nothing here reopens it.
  • Measurement notes: emotion values are single-token, so the first-token and full-string read-outs are bit-identical here (both recorded). Isometry r saturates near 0.99 at every swept layer and is useless as a layer selector; the per-layer Spearman of the recovered ordering is the better tiebreaker, and it is what flagged the valence line's worst layer. The 3D views show the first three PCA coordinates of a higher-dimensional fit — faithful projection, not the whole space.

What's next

Three follow-ups, all cheap and well-posed:

next · 01 A pullback leg on arousal The lab's gradient pullback optimizes the activation that induces a target behavior, on-device. Ask it to hit each intermediate emotion; check whether the optimized path rides the fitted manifold. The bidirectional version of this result.
next · 02 A circumplex fit for valence Two intrinsic coordinates (valence × arousal — the standard psychological model) instead of one, so the walk can go around the calm midpoint instead of through it. The fitter already supports cyclic concepts.
next · 03 A second seed, varied prompts Minutes of compute on the arousal walk. Turns 1.00-vs-0.83 into a number with an error bar.
· · ·

The result that holds: emotion lines are as geometrically clean in Qwen3.5-2B's residual stream as the best ordinal concepts this lab has measured, and on the one axis without a sign flip, the path structure of that geometry is behaviorally real, waypoint by waypoint, against both controls.

The result that does not: this is not a general win for manifold over linear steering on emotions. One axis routes; one collapses at a geometric obstruction the parameterization cannot represent; one never moves at all. The geometry is cheap to fit. Knowing when it buys control is the research question, and the failures above are half of the answer.
