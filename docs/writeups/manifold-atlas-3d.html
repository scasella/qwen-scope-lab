TL;DR

Following the centroid-routing result on the arousal axis, we asked the cheap question: how many more concepts trace clean manifolds in this model? Of twenty new candidates — physical scales, abstract scales, two cyclic — twelve are clean (order recovery ≥ 0.9 and isometry r ≥ 0.9), eight partial, none diffuse. Clean geometry is common and cheap to find.

Turning that geometry into stepwise control is the rare part. Re-running the routing test on the eleven clean ordinals, only two clear the strict gate — and weakly (agreement 0.33–0.50) next to arousal's 1.00. The honest headline: clean geometry is necessary but not sufficient for control. That gap is exactly what a stiffness predictor would have to explain.

The census

Each candidate is an ordered set of values with carrier templates. We fit the residual-stream manifold at several layers, then score it two ways: order recovery — does the principal axis reproduce the concept's ordering (abs-Spearman for ordinal, ring-adjacency for cyclic) — and isometry r, the paper's activation↔behavior correspondence. A concept is clean if, at its best layer, both clear 0.9. This is a geometry measurement; it makes no steering claim.

The partials are informative: most (pain, politeness, planets, belt-rank) have isometry r ≥ 0.96 but imperfect order recovery in the top PCA axes — clean shape, slightly tangled ordering. The only genuinely weak fits are the two small-n cyclic concepts (seasons, 4 points; moon-phases, 5) — too few points to close a ring, the same effect that left compass and months diffuse in the original atlas.

Clean geometry, rarely clean control

The manifold being clean says the model represents the concept on an ordered curve. Whether you can walk that curve and make the model step through the intermediate values — centroid routing, the arousal result — is a separate question. We re-ran the routing test (manifold vs linear chord, against shuffled-order and random-direction controls) on all eleven clean ordinals at their best layer, with the same generic prompts used to fit them.

Two clear the strict gate — age_stage and certainty — and even they route weakly (0.33 and 0.50 agreement) compared with arousal's clean 1.00. Most clean-geometry concepts walk like the linear chord: they snap to the endpoint instead of stepping through. A caveat sharpens this rather than rescuing it: these used the generic carrier prompts that fit the geometry, not prompts tuned to elicit each value as a continuation — so the routing numbers are a floor, and prompt-tuning is one of the things a follow-up would vary. But the pattern is the finding.

Why this is the useful result

If clean geometry guaranteed control, you could read isometry r off a census and ship a knob. It doesn't. The decision is "fit is cheap, routability is the scarce thing" — which is the precise motivation for a transverse-stiffness predictor: a geometric signature computed before steering that says whether a given clean manifold will route. Arousal routes, age_stage and certainty route weakly, priority (the cleanest fit of all) doesn't route at these prompts. Three labeled points became thirteen.

Bounds on the claim

Honest scope
  • Does say: twelve new concepts form clean residual-stream manifolds on real Qwen3.5-2B (order recovery and isometry both ≥ 0.9 at their best layer); the 3D figures are the actual fitted PCA-3 geometry, not illustrations.
  • Does not say: that these are steering knobs — only two of eleven tested clear the routing gate, weakly, and with un-tuned prompts; nor that the geometry is fully 3-dimensional (the plots show the first three PCA coordinates of a higher-dimensional fit).
  • Measurement notes: single seed, single model; best layer chosen per concept by order recovery; routing uses the full-string read-out, six waypoints, generic carrier prompts. Full per-layer sweeps and per-waypoint walks in reports/manifold_census/.

What's next

The census did its job: it turned a three-concept result into a thirteen-concept labeled set of (geometry, routability) pairs. That is the training data for the predictor — fit transverse variance, midpoint curvature, and the sign-flip detector against routes-or-doesn't, and you get a go/no-go certificate before spending on a steer. The promoted concepts also drop straight into the lab's Manifold mode, so any of these twelve can be walked and watched in the GUI.

· · ·

The result that holds: the residual stream of a 2-billion-parameter model is densely organized into clean concept geometries — twelve more, found in one afternoon's census, none diffuse. The map is real and rich.

The result that doesn't: a clean map is not a steering wheel. Reading the geometry is cheap; driving along it is concept-dependent and, so far, rare. The next question isn't "are there manifolds" — there are, everywhere — it's "which ones can you move along," and the failures above are the start of that answer.
