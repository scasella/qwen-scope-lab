AI safety · the cheap detector

A tiny, free detector caught jailbreak attempts it had never seen before

On a small open AI model, one cheap calculation on numbers the model already computes spotted manipulation attempts across attack styles it was never trained on — matching a paid AI judge, and beating the method built for the job.

Model: Qwen3.5-2B, a small open model Detector: one calculation on the model's own activity Cost: essentially free per message

Every company that runs an AI chatbot has the same nuisance: people try to talk it out of its own rules. These attempts — known as "jailbreaks," messages crafted to make an AI ignore its safety training — are common enough that catching them has become its own small industry. The usual defense is to put a second AI in front of the first, or pay an outside service to screen every incoming message. Both cost money, and both add a delay to every conversation.

Work on a small, freely available open model just showed that a far cheaper option holds up about as well. Instead of a second AI, it reads the first AI's own internal activity — the numbers it already produces while processing your message — and runs a single calculation on them. That calculation flagged jailbreak attempts as accurately as a paid AI judge, beat the specialized tool built for the job, and caught attack styles it had never been shown.

What they actually built

The idea fits in one picture. As a model reads your message, it lights up a vast array of internal numbers, its "activations." The researchers took a handful of example messages — some jailbreaks, some ordinary requests — and found the single direction through those numbers that best separates the two groups. After that, screening a new message is one multiplication: does it lean toward the "manipulation" side, or the "ordinary" side? No second model. No outside call. The detector rides along inside a model already running.

One multiplication
That's the whole detector. It reads numbers the model computes anyway, so screening each message adds almost nothing — no second AI, no per-message API bill.

It matched the paid version — and it had never seen these attacks

On a clean test, the free calculation told every jailbreak apart from every ordinary request. So did a paid AI judge — OpenAI's GPT-4o-mini, asked to rate each message — which scored just as perfectly. The specialized tool built for this job — software that picks out the tidy, nameable concepts buried inside a model — trailed well behind. A random guess landed at chance, where it belongs.

The part that stood out came next. The detector had been trained only on obvious attacks — "pretend you have no rules," "ignore your previous instructions." Then it faced six completely different tricks it had never seen: a sob story about a late grandmother, a request smuggled inside computer code, a fake "just finish this sentence" trap. It caught all of them. It hadn't memorized the wording of attacks. It had learned, from the inside, what manipulation looks like.

Then came the trick questions

Here is the part most write-ups would skip, and it is the most useful one. A real deployment never sees tidy attacks and tidy innocent messages. It sees innocent messages dressed up to look like attacks — "explain how prompt injection works for my security class" — and real attacks stripped of every obvious tell. The researchers built exactly those, then ran the detector again without touching its settings.

The good news held: it still sorted attacks from innocent messages almost perfectly. The catch is where it draws the line. At the cutoff it shipped with, about 1 in 4 innocent-but-suspicious messages tripped a false alarm, and about 1 in 4 of the sneakiest disguised attacks slipped through.

The detector's judgment was sound; its factory setting was off. Re-tuning that one cutoff on a given service's real traffic fixes most of it, but it means the free detector is not quite plug-and-play. The hard test also showed why the plain calculation keeps winning. The specialized tool leans on a few tidy, nameable concepts, and the messiest disguised messages fall between them, so it came apart here. The plain calculation uses the model's whole internal pattern at once, so it barely moved, and stayed level with the paid AI judge.

Why it matters

Strip it down and the takeaway is cheap to act on. If you run a model and can read its internal activity, you can stand up a first-line jailbreak detector for almost nothing: screen every message with no second AI and no per-message bill, and catch attack styles nobody wrote down in advance. What you cannot do is trust the factory cutoff. You have to tune it on your own traffic first.

That last sentence is the honest edge of the result, and it is as useful as the headline. A free detector that quietly false-alarms on a quarter of suspicious-looking but harmless messages would burn user trust fast if it shipped blind. Knowing exactly where it slips is what makes it safe to use — and what separates a real finding from a press release.

Fine print

The numbers come from one small open model (Qwen3.5-2B) read at a single internal layer, on one run, with small test batches: eight example messages per category, and eight per trick on the hard test. Small samples make the exact percentages rough.

"As accurately as a paid AI judge" means both told attacks from ordinary messages without a miss on the clean test; on the hardest disguised attacks the paid judge stayed a hair ahead of the free one.

The hard-test attacks and innocent look-alikes were written by the researchers, not pulled from a real-world jailbreak database or a live red-team trying to beat the detector on purpose. So the next step is testing it against attacks in the wild, and against an attacker who can see the detector and aims at it. None of that is buried; it's the edge of what the evidence actually shows.

Based on results logged on the Qwen Scope Lab, May 2026; every figure traces to a recorded run on rented GPUs, with the paid AI judge checked beforehand to confirm it could tell a clear attack from a clear ordinary message.
Terminology, once: the "free calculation" is a difference-of-means linear probe over the model's residual stream; the "specialized tool" is a sparse-autoencoder feature monitor; "told attacks from ordinary messages" refers to area-under-ROC on a held-out split.
